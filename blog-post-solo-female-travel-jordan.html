Solo Female Travel Tips in Jordan: A Local's Guide

Published on February 4, 2026 (Updated February 5, 2026)

Jordan is a land of ancient wonders, Bedouin generosity, and stark, beautiful landscapes. While it is generally safe and incredibly welcoming, solo female travelers often have unique questions about navigating its deeply traditional culture. Most locals are incredibly kind and proud to share their heritage, but cultural awareness is key to a smooth and respectful journey.

This isn’t just a "relaxing beach holiday"—it’s an adventure. Stay alert, respect the traditions, and let Jordan’s magic surprise you. Here are five essential tips for solo female travelers in Jordan.

1. Modesty Matters: Respect Local Customs

Dress conservatively to respect local customs. While covering your hair isn’t required everywhere, loose clothing that covers shoulders and knees is highly appreciated. Blending in earns respect and often leads to more authentic interactions with locals.

In more touristy areas like Petra, Amman, or Aqaba, you’ll see diverse attire, but we recommend staying modest to feel more comfortable. Always carry a scarf in your bag—it's essential for visiting mosques like the King Hussein Mosque in Amman or the Sharif Hussein Bin Ali Mosque in Aqaba.

Local Tip: Linen trousers and long, loose tunics are your best friends in the Jordanian heat. They keep you cool while remaining culturally appropriate.

2. Bargain Smartly and Confirm Prices

Haggling is a common part of life in the souks (markets) and when dealing with taxi drivers. To avoid misunderstandings, always confirm prices before accepting any services, whether it’s a taxi ride from Amman to Jerash or a camel ride in the majestic Wadi Rum.

Avoid paying upfront for tours. Instead, use licensed guides recommended by your accommodation or reputable local experience providers. This ensures you get a fair price and a quality experience.

3. Stay Aware in Public Spaces

Jordan is generally safe, but like anywhere else, it’s important to stay aware of your surroundings. Avoid isolated areas after dark and trust your instincts—if a situation feels off, don't hesitate to walk away.

Jordanian men are often very hospitable, but it's important to set clear boundaries to avoid misunderstandings. Politely decline persistent offers for “private tours” or invitations from strangers if you feel uncomfortable.

4. Emotional Resilience and Basic Arabic

Outside of the main tourist hubs, you might feel overwhelmed by stares or curiosity. It’s okay to feel uneasy—Jordan’s culture is warm but deeply traditional. Learning a few basic Arabic phrases can go a long way in navigating these situations with grace.

Use the phrase “لا شكراً” (la shukran – “No, thank you”) to politely but firmly deflect unwanted attention. A simple smile and a "la shukran" are usually enough to move on respectfully.

5. Choose Accommodation Wisely

Opt for private rooms in hostels (common in Amman and Aqaba) or reputable guesthouses where you can meet other travelers while having your own space. Locals are friendly but may not always share the same concept of personal space—don't be afraid to politely set your boundaries.

For a truly peaceful experience, consider booking eco-camps in Wadi Rum or seeking out women-run stays and cooperatives. These often provide a unique perspective on Jordanian life and support local women's initiatives.

Overall Advice: Jordan demands cultural awareness, not fear. Respect traditions (such as declining alcohol during Ramadan) and avoid sensitive political discussions. If you approach the country with an open heart and a respectful attitude, you will find that Jordanians are some of the most hospitable people in the world.
